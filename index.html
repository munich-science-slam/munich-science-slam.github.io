Science Needs Speakers

Scientists need to be able to clearly and precisely communicate their findings in order to advance their personal careers, advance the scientific knowledge, and advance public awareness to increase scientific support and help inform decision and policy makers. Join us in an open, relaxed and pleasant atmosphere where we will practice giving engaging scientific talks! Sign up by email: munichscienceslam@gmail.com

Schedule

  • The Week Before: Pre-Slam presentation skills workshop

    • Time: Wednesday, October 10th @ 6:00pm-10pm
    • Venue: Google Munich

  • "Securing Your Take-Home Message"

    Learn to build a version of your talk that highlights the main points of your story by creating a talk structure that maximizes your presentation’s impact within the time allowed for it.

  • “Building Attractive Slides”

    Visuals matter! Learn strategies for making nice and meaningful slides that augment, rather than distract from, your presentation.

  • “Engaging Your Audience by Knowing Your Goals”

    It is not just what you say that matters, it’s also the way you say it! Learn how to adjust phrasing, body language, and talk format to better-impact your audience.

  • “Becoming Fully Prepared: The Double-Time Method”

    How do you become ready for a presentation and still seem natural and spontaneous? We’ll polish your presentation using an exercise that enhances your ability to stay natural!

  • The Main Event: Munich Science Slam!

    • Time: Friday, October 19th @ 6:30pm-10pm
    • Venue: Jetbrains, Elsenheimerstraße 47, Munich

  • 2-Minutes Lightning Talks

    The speakers will enthuse us and engage us with stories of their research!

  • 10-Minute Full Talks

    More speakers will grab us and engage us with stories of their discoveries!

  • Break Time: Food & Drinks!

Organizers



Alexandra Klein

Alexandra was born and raised in Bavaria and studied in Munich and Berlin. Currently, she is a doctoral candidate at the Max Planck Institute of Neurobiology in Munich. In her research she aims at understanding how the brain creates and regulates emotions. Her heart beats for communicating the amazing world of (neuro)science to people who are not necessarily as nerdy as she is.


Nicholas A. Del Grosso

An American systems neuroscience doctoral candidate at Ludwig-Maximilian-Universität in Munich, Germany. Nick researches closed-loop technologies for the purpose of understanding and modifying brain function. Passionate about empowering scientists and engineers through programming and data science training, Nick has six years of experience organizing workshops, courses, and training communities in Germany and abroad.
